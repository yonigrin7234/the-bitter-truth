Section 01

Every mile protects the brain. No app in the world shows a runner that.

Strava tracks pace. Nike Run Club tracks streaks. Garmin tracks heart rate zones. No running app anywhere translates physical activity into its effect on the brain — despite a decade of published evidence quantifying exactly that relationship.

The Framingham Heart Study found that physical activity reduces dementia risk by up to 45%. Nature Medicine 2025 confirmed that exercise directly slows tau protein accumulation. Johns Hopkins 2025 established that there is no minimum threshold — any activity provides measurable cognitive benefit.

That means every run a user logs has a scientifically grounded brain health impact that can be estimated, tracked, and shown to them. The Foundation's events — the LA Marathon, the Pasadena Half Marathon, the Santa Monica Classic — become activation moments within a year-round relationship, not the entire relationship.

Section 03

Wired into the events the Foundation already runs

The platform is not a standalone app. It connects directly to the Foundation's event infrastructure — race registration, chip timing, and year-over-year participation history. Runners enter the brain health ecosystem the moment they register. Their training data feeds the model for months before race day. Their finish feeds it on race day. Their continued activity feeds it for the months and years after. Every returning participant builds a longitudinal brain health record that grows more valuable with each event.

Register
Account created. Apple Health, Google Fit, Garmin, Strava, Whoop, Oura, and Fitbit connected. Baseline brain health score calculated.
Train
Weekly activity feeds the cognitive reserve model. Monthly brain health summaries. Training milestones tied to neuroprotection data.
Race Day
Live splits from chip timing. Collective impact dashboard: 25,000 runners generating 112,000 hours of neuroprotective activity.
Post-Race
Finish summary with cumulative brain health contribution. Year-over-year comparison for returning runners.
Off-Season
Continued activity tracking. Research updates. Foundation content. Nudges for upcoming events.
Next Event
Pasadena Half registration prompt based on activity level. Longitudinal trajectory grows with each race.

A runner who completes the LA Marathon in 2027 and returns for the Pasadena Half later that year has 10+ months of continuous brain health data. By the 2028 LA Marathon, the app shows them an 18-month trajectory — not a snapshot, but a trend. That longitudinal view is the retention mechanism. It is also, with proper consent and IRB approval, the beginning of a population-level research dataset that the Foundation's partners at MGH and Brigham and Women's can build on.

Methodology

The brain health model — translating published population-level hazard ratios into individual-level estimates — will be developed in collaboration with the Foundation's research partners and published as a methodology document. This is what separates the platform from a wellness score. The science is the product.

Section 04

What the Foundation gains

365
Year-Round Relationship

The Foundation currently touches a marathon participant for 4–6 hours once a year. The app makes it 365 days.

Every interaction reinforces the Foundation's brand, its science, and its mission. Push notifications for new research findings. Monthly brain health summaries. Event countdown integrations. The runner never forgets who is behind the platform.

200K+
Direct Audience

The Foundation's events reach hundreds of thousands of runners annually. Right now, there is no owned channel to reach them after they cross the finish line.

The app creates a first-party audience the Foundation controls — not dependent on social media algorithms, email open rates, or media coverage. That audience is a donor pipeline, a research recruitment pool, and a public health messaging platform.

$0
Donor Conversion Engine

"We have 200,000 active users tracking the brain health impact of their physical activity, powered by the research we fund." That is a sentence that raises money.

The app gives the Foundation a tangible product to show donors — not a grant report, not a white paper, but a living platform with active users and measurable impact. That is a fundamentally different fundraising conversation.

IRB
Research Data Asset

Aggregated, anonymized physical activity data from hundreds of thousands of runners, correlated with the brain health model. MGH and Brigham would find that dataset genuinely valuable.

With opt-in consent and IRB approval, the app becomes a passive longitudinal data collection instrument. The Foundation moves from funding other people's research to generating its own data — a permanent upgrade in institutional capability.

Section 05

How it gets built

Months 1–6
Phase 1 — Foundation
  • Integration with Apple Health, Google Fit, Garmin, Strava, Whoop, Oura, and Fitbit
  • Brain health metric based on published Framingham hazard ratios
  • Event integration: LA Marathon, Pasadena, Santa Monica
  • Scientific citations visible and linked for every calculation
  • iOS and Android
Months 7–12
Phase 2 — Community
  • Foundation events and Neurological Symposium content feed
  • Aggregate impact dashboards (collective miles, collective risk reduction)
  • Research updates and new study notifications
  • Social sharing of brain health milestones (not leaderboards — the science says consistency matters more than peak performance)
Year 2
Phase 3 — Research
  • Opt-in anonymized data sharing with research partners
  • IRB-approved data pipeline to MGH and Brigham
  • Longitudinal tracking features for returning participants across multiple race years
  • Integration with Foundation's broader neurological disease research agenda

Phase 1 is a focused build — an iOS and Android app with a clear thesis, clean execution, and no feature bloat. It proves the concept. Phases 2 and 3 expand only after the core product demonstrates engagement. Estimated build cost for Phase 1: $300K–$500K with an external development partner. Annual maintenance: $100K–$150K.

The question is not whether the science supports it.

The evidence is published. The events exist. The partnerships are operational.

The question is whether the Foundation wants to be a grantmaker that funds other people's platforms — or the organization that builds its own.
